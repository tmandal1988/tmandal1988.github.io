Human Pilot Research

Sponsor: NASA Langley Research Center, NASA Aviation Safety Program (AvSP), Vehicle Systems Safety Technologies (VSST) Project.

The objective of the project is to develop information fusion based tools for real-time monitoring and prediction of the Pilot Vehicle System (PVS) closed-loop stability. Modern aircraft systems have a highly complex closed-loop starting from sensors, signal processing, displaying and cueing, human pilot, flight control, signal distribution, to actuators, which finally control the aircraft. Current aviation safety research has emphasized the modeling aspect of individual sub-systems within this link. However, a time-varying pilot control model is needed for deriving the closed-loop PVS describing function. This would in turn help us to evaluate PVS stability margins, to predict unsafe flight conditions, and to develop mitigation approaches. Additionally, an improved understanding of low-level human control strategies will help us to better evaluate the impact of new technologies, such as adaptive sensing and control algorithms, on the safety of future aircraft systems.

Estimation technique based on Unscented Kalman Filter has been developed to estimate the parameters of the pilot model online for single input single output paradigm (pitch control of an aircraft). The goal is to show that estimator is robust for linear modelling errors and small non-linearity and that the estimator is able to correctly predict the frequency characteristics of the PVS to calculate stability margins.

For large non-linear error and application domain an estimator based on particle filter is being developed. Particle filter is computationally expensive but can better take care of non-linearity. It can still provide a proof of concept for online pilot model parameter estimation for complex conditions. This research work has inspired me even more to ask questions such as how one can make autonomous robots which are flexible, robust, adaptive, and capable of solving various kinds of problem at the same time?

Pilot Research Aircraft (PIRAT)

PIRAT is an off-the-shelf remotely controlled sub-scale aircraft, which has been modified to carry out various experiments involving human pilot. Being a mass produced off-the-shelf platform, PIRAT is inexpensive and easily replaceable. Using unmanned aircraft does have the disadvantage of not being able to provide motion cues to the pilot on the ground. However use of unmanned aircraft provides the flexibility to carry out dangerous flight tests without putting human life in danger and in a cost effective way. Also since human pilot uses only vision feedback to fly the aircraft, it resembles the single input single output system used in human pilot research.

PIRAT is equipped with Gen-VI avionics developed at Interactive Robotics Laboratory at West Virginia University. Gen-VI avionics consists of a Netburner© MOD54415 32-bit 250 MHz microcontroller. It has 64MB DDR2 RAM and 32MB flash which enables the designer to run basic flight test algorithms (such as time delay injection in pilot command and actuator rate limiting) along with low level tasks such as gathering sensor data and controlling servos. Gen VI avionics also has a PC104 form factor computer to run real time linux for RTAI flight programs. In terms of major sensors Gen-VI consists of an Analog Devices© ADIS16485 Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) for measuring 3-axis accelerations and angular rates. A Specktrun© AR12120 R/C flight control receiver to receive and record pilot command, and a Novatel© OEMV-1 GPS module. Gen-VI avionics can operate in manual, fully autonomous or Pilot-In-The-Loop mode of flight making PIRAT a versatile platform for various types of research.

I lead a group of three undergraduate students and one master's student for developing and flight testing Pilot Research Aircraft (PIRAT). Firmware for Gen-VI avionics can be obtained from my github repo.

NASA Centennial (Sample Return Robot) Challenge

The main idea of Sample Return Robot (SRR) Challenge was to encourage innovators to build fully-autonomous robots that can find, retrieve, and return samples in a large (~80,000m2) complex environment without relying on Earth based technologies (e.g. GPS or magnetometer). The challenge has two levels: $5,000 Level-1 Challenge for picking up one sample and up to $1.5M Level-2 Challenge for picking up (up to) 10 different samples. Only teams that completed Level-1 Challenge will be eligible for the second level.

Year 2014

We (Team Mountaineers) became the only team that completed Level-1 Challenge in 2014 (NASA press release, NASA 360 TV)! Our story from Year 2014 can be found here. As a part of the firmware team I developed codes for Netburner© modules for multiple sensor data collection with emphasis on fault tolerant and synchronization built in. I also developed the firmware for the grabber mechanisam of the robot for sample collection.

Year 2015

After another year of hard work, we completed Level-2 Challenge in 2015 ( NASA press release) and brought home a $100,000 prize! Our story from Year 2015 can be found here. As a part of the firmware team I helped develop general fimware for the robot such as UDP communication and range measurements using Time Doman© ranging radio.

Quadrotor

Autonomous navigation in an unstructured environment, as an essential requirement for applications such as search and rescue or exploration of planetary surfaces, has long been a challenging research topic in the robotics community. Within this project, quadrotor platforms were developed (due to ease of flight testing and rapid prototyping) to test various navigation, sensor fusion, path planning, and flight control techniques in unstructured and GPS degraded environment. I was responsible for developing firmware for attitude and position control and sensor fusion and as a part of the research I helped in the research with path-planning, navigation, and flight testing.
